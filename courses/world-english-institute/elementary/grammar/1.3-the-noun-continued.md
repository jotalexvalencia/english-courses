`DE NAUN (CON-TI-NIUD)`

**INTRODUCTION**
`IN-TRO-DAK-SHON`

**As stated in Lessons One and Two, a noun is a word that names a person, place, or thing.**
`As stei-ted in Le-sons Uan and Tu, a naun is a word dat neims a per-son, pleis, or ting.`

**You have learned that there are various kinds of nouns: common and proper nouns, collective nouns, concrete and abstract nouns, and specific and non-specific nouns.**
`Yu hav lernd dat der ar ve-ri-os kainds ov nauns: co-mon and pro-per nauns, co-lec-tiv nauns, con-crit and abs-tract nauns, and spe-ci-fic and non-spe-ci-fic nauns.`

**You have also learned how to change a noun from singular to plural and how to make a noun possessive.** **(posesivo)**
`Yu hav ol-so lernd hau tu cheinj a naun from sin-giu-lar tu plu-ral and hau tu meik a naun po-se-siv.`

**In this lesson, we will learn that all nouns have four qualities known as "properties": (1) gender, (2) person, (3) number, and (4) case.** **(propiedades)** **(género)** **(persona - gramatical)** **(número - gramatical)** **(caso - gramatical)**
`In dis le-son, wi wil lern dat ol nauns hav for cua-li-tis noun as "pro-per-tis": (uan) yen-der, (tu) per-son, (tri) nom-ber, and (for) keis.`

**NOUNS TO LEARN**
`NAUNS TU LERN`

**1. "boundary" (noun) A border or a limit.** **(límite, frontera)**
`uan. "baun-da-ri" (naun) A bor-der or a li-mit.`

**Sentence: "God has put boundaries around Satan."**
`Sen-tens: "God has put baun-da-ris a-raund Sei-tan."`

**2. "case" (noun) A characteristic or form of a word that indicates the relation of that word to other words in the sentence.** **(caso - gramatical)**
`tu. "keis" (naun) A ca-rac-te-ris-tic or form ov a word dat in-di-keits de ri-lei-shon ov dat word tu o-der words in de sen-tens.`

**Sentence: "The subject of a sentence is always in the Nominative Case."**
`Sen-tens: "De sob-yect ov a sen-tens is ol-weis in de No-mi-na-tiv Keis."`

**3. "descendant" (noun) An offspring.** **(descendiente)** **(cría, vástago)**
`tri. "di-sen-dant" (naun) An of-spring.`

**Sentence: "Every human being is a descendant of Adam and Eve."**
`Sen-tens: "Ev-ri hiu-man bi-ing is a di-sen-dant ov A-dam and Iv."`

**4. "gender" (noun) A characteristic or form of a word indicating male sex, female sex, or neither sex.** **(género - gramatical)**
`for. "yen-der" (naun) A ca-rac-te-ris-tic or form ov a word in-di-kei-ting meil sex, fi-meil sex, or nai-der sex.`

**For example, the word "stallion" is masculine in gender, and the word "mare" is feminine.** **(semental, caballo)** **(yegua)**
`For eg-zam-pol, de word "sta-lion" is mas-kiu-lin in yen-der, and de word "mer" is fe-mi-nin.`

**5. "number" (noun) A characteristic or form of a word indicating whether the word refers to one or more than one.** **(número - gramatical)**
`faiv. "nom-ber" (naun) A ca-rac-te-ris-tic or form ov a word in-di-kei-ting we-der de word ri-fers tu uan or mor dan uan.`

**Sentence: "The word 'man' is singular in number, but the word 'men' is plural."**
`Sen-tens: "De word 'man' is sin-giu-lar in nom-ber, bat de word 'men' is plu-ral."`

**6. "temptation" (noun) Something that leads, invites, or draws a person into evil** **(tentación)**
`siks. "temp-tei-shon" (naun) Som-ting dat lids, in-vaits, or dros a per-son in-tu i-vol`

**Sentence: "When we are tempted, God gives us a way to escape that temptation."**
`Sen-tens: "Wen wi ar temp-ted, God givs as a wei tu es-keip dat temp-tei-shon."`

`Sen-tens: "De Bi-bli-cal sto-ri a-baut A-dam and Iv is a fa-si-nei-ting sto-ri."`

**PROPERTIES OF NOUNS** **(Propiedades de los Sustantivos)**
`PRO-PER-TIS OV NAUNS`

**All nouns have four characteristics called "properties":**
`Ol nauns hav for ca-rac-te-ris-tics cold "pro-per-tis":`

**(1) gender,**
`(uan) yen-der,`

**(2) person,**
`(tu) per-son,`

**(3) number, and**
`(tri) nom-ber, and`

**(4) case.**
`(for) keis.`

**Let us look at these properties one by one.**
`Let as luk at dis pro-per-tis uan bai uan.`

**1. Gender, in the English language, is "natural," not grammatical.**
`uan. Yen-der, in de Ing-glish lan-güey, is "na-chu-ral," not gra-ma-ti-cal.`

**That is, the gender of a noun is determined not by the grammatical form of the word but by the sex of the person, place, or thing that is named.**
`Dat is, de yen-der ov a naun is di-ter-mind not bai de gra-ma-ti-cal form ov de word bat bai de sex ov de per-son, pleis, or ting dat is neimd.`

**Gender, in English, "denotes" male sex, female sex, "either" sex, or "neither" sex.** **(denota, indica)** 
`Yen-der, in Ing-glish, di-nouts meil sex, fi-meil sex, ai-der sex, or nai-der sex.`

**Masculine gender denotes the male sex.**
`Mas-kiu-lin yen-der di-nouts de meil sex.`

**Examples of masculine nouns are as follows: boy, brother, bull, cock, colt, father, stallion, husband, king, man, son, and uncle.** **(toro)** **(gallo)** **(potro)** **(semental)**
`Eg-zam-pols ov mas-kiu-lin nauns ar as fo-lous: boi, bro-der, bul, cok, colt, fa-der, sta-lion, hos-band, king, man, son, and on-col.`

**Feminine gender denotes the female sex.**
`Fe-mi-nin yen-der di-nouts de fi-meil sex.`

**Examples of feminine nouns are as follows: girl, sister, cow, hen, filly, mother, mare, wife, queen, woman, daughter, and aunt.** **(vaca)** **(gallina)** **(potra)** **(yegua)**
`Eg-zam-pols ov fe-mi-nin nauns ar as fo-lous: gerl, sis-ter, cau, hen, fi-li, mo-der, mer, waif, cuin, wu-man, do-ter, and ant.`

**Common gender denotes either sex.**
`Co-mon yen-der di-nouts ai-der sex.`

**Examples of common gender nouns are: birds, cattle, children, citizens, driver, friend, lawyer, neighbor, parent, relative, singer, and worker.** **(ganado)** **(ciudadanos)** **(abogado)** **(vecino)** **(pariente)**
`Eg-zam-pols ov co-mon yen-der nauns ar: berds, ca-tel, chil-dren, ci-ti-sens, drai-ver, frend, lo-yer, nei-bor, pa-rent, re-la-tiv, sin-ger, and wor-ker.`

**Neuter gender denotes neither male nor female sex.** **(neutro)**
`Niu-ter yen-der di-nouts nai-der meil nor fi-meil sex.`

**Examples of neuter gender nouns are as follows: books, cabinet, city, desk, house, ink, lamp, pen, river, tree.** **(gabinete, armario)** **(tinta)**
`Eg-zam-pols ov niu-ter yen-der nauns ar as fo-lous: buks, ca-bi-net, ci-ti, desk, haus, ink, lamp, pen, ri-ver, tri.`

**2. "Person" is a characteristic or form that lets us know whether the word relates to the speaker, to the person spoken to, or to the person or thing spoken about.**
`tu. "Per-son" is a ca-rac-te-ris-tic or form dat lets as nou we-der de word ri-leits tu de spi-ker, tu de per-son spo-ken tu, or tu de per-son or ting spo-ken a-baut.`

**A first-person noun refers to a person who is doing the speaking.**
`A ferst-per-son naun ri-fers tu a per-son hu is du-ing de spi-king.`

**Example: "I, John, was on the island of Patmos" (Revelation 1:9).**
`Eg-zam-pol: "Ai, Yon, was on de ai-land ov Pat-mos" (Re-ve-lei-shon uan nain).`

**In this sentence, the noun "John" is in the first person because John is the speaker.**
`In dis sen-tens, de naun "Yon" is in de ferst per-son bi-cos Yon is de spi-ker.`

**A second-person noun refers to a person who is being addressed.** **(dirigido, a quien se habla)**
`A se-cond-per-son naun ri-fers tu a per-son hu is bi-ing a-drest.`

**Example: "Friends, Romans, and countrymen, lend me your ears."** **(compatriotas)** **(prestadme atención / oídos)**
`Eg-zam-pol: "Frends, Ro-mans, and con-tri-men, lend mi yur irs."`

**In this famous speech from Julius Caesar, the nouns "friends," "Romans," and "countrymen" are in the second person because they name people who are being spoken to.**
`In dis fei-mos spich from Yu-li-os Si-sar, de nauns "frends," "Ro-mans," and "con-tri-men" ar in de se-cond per-son bi-cos dey neim pi-pol hu ar bi-ing spo-ken tu.`

**A third-person noun refers to a person or thing being spoken about.**
`A terd-per-son naun ri-fers tu a per-son or ting bi-ing spo-ken a-baut.`

**Example: "In the beginning, God created the earth and sky."**
`Eg-zam-pol: "In de bi-gi-ning, God cri-ei-ted de erz and skai."`

**The noun "God" is third person because he is the person being discussed.**
`De naun "God" is terd per-son bi-cos hi is de per-son bi-ing dis-cost.`

**3. "Number" is something in the form of a noun that tells us whether the noun refers to only one person, place, or thing or more than one.**
`tri. "Nom-ber" is som-ting in de form ov a naun dat tels as we-der de naun ri-fers tu on-li uan per-son, pleis, or ting or mor dan uan.`

**The singular noun names only one person, place, or thing.**
`De sin-giu-lar naun neims on-li uan per-son, pleis, or ting.`

**The plural noun names more than one person, place, or thing.]**
`De plu-ral naun neims mor dan uan per-son, pleis, or ting.`

**Some nouns do not have a plural form.**
`Som nauns du not hav a plu-ral form.`

**These nouns are called "non-count" nouns.** **(incontables)**
`Dis nauns ar cold "non-caunt" nauns.`

**Material things like bread, butter, coffee, gold, oxygen, silver, sugar, and water are not counted.**
`Ma-ti-ri-al tings laik bred, bo-ter, co-fi, gold, ok-si-yen, sil-ver, shu-gar, and wo-ter ar not caun-ted.`

**Instead, they are measured.** **(medidos)**
`Ins-ted, dey ar me-shurd.`

**We do not say, "Give me two breads."**
`Wi du not sei, "Giv mi tu breds."`

**We say, "Give me two loaves of bread," or "two slices of bread."** **(hogazas)** **(rebanadas)**
`Wi sei, "Giv mi tu louvs ov bred," or "tu slai-ses ov bred."`

**Abstract nouns are non-count nouns.**
`Abs-trakt nauns ar non-caunt nauns.`

**Abstract nouns include concepts like freedom and liberty; emotions like love, greed, and hate; fields of study like biology, chemistry, and engineering; qualities like beauty, excellence, and peace; and recreational activities like gardening, swimming, and tennis.** **(libertad)** **(codicia)** **(odio)** **(campos de estudio)** **(excelencia)** **(jardinería)**
`Abs-trakt nauns in-clud con-septs laik fri-dom and li-ber-ti; i-mo-shons laik lov, grid, and heit; filds ov sta-di laik bai-o-lo-yi, ke-mis-tri, and en-yi-ni-ring; cua-li-tis laik biu-ti, ek-se-lens, and pis; and re-cri-ei-sho-nal ak-ti-vi-tis laik gar-de-ning, sui-ming, and te-nis.`

**None of these abstract nouns has a plural form.**
`Non ov dis abs-trakt nauns has a plu-ral form.`

**1. "Case" is the relation of a noun or pronoun to other words in the sentence.**
`uan. "Keis" is de ri-lei-shon ov a naun or pro-naun tu o-der words in de sen-tens.`

**English nouns have three cases:**
`Ing-glish nauns hav tri kei-ses:`

**(a) Nominative,**
`(ei) No-mi-na-tiv,`

**(b) Objective, and**
`(bi) Ob-yek-tiv, and`

**(c) Possessive.**
`(si) Po-se-siv.`

**a. The nominative case uses a noun (or pronoun) as the subject of a sentence or as the predicate following a linking verb.** **(verbo copulativo)**
`ei. De no-mi-na-tiv keis iu-ses a naun (or pro-naun) as de sob-yekt ov a sen-tens or as de pre-di-keit fo-lo-wing a lin-king verb.`

**("Nominative" means "pertaining to a name."** **(perteneciente a)** **See the Introductory Lesson concerning the "subject" and "predicate.")**
`("No-mi-na-tiv" mins "per-tei-ning tu a neim." Si de In-tro-dak-to-ri Le-son con-ser-ning de "sob-yekt" and "pre-di-keit.")`

**When a noun (or pronoun) is the subject of a sentence, it is called the "subject nominative."**
`Wen a naun (or pro-naun) is de sob-yekt ov a sen-tens, it is cold de "sob-yekt no-mi-na-tiv."`

**Example: "The moon is shining."**
`Eg-zam-pol: "De mun is shai-ning."`

**In this sentence, "moon" is the subject nominative.**
`In dis sen-tens, "mun" is de sob-yekt no-mi-na-tiv.`

**When a noun (or pronoun) is the predicate of the sentence following a linking verb, it is called the "predicate nominative."**
`Wen a naun (or pro-naun) is de pre-di-keit ov de sen-tens fo-lo-wing a lin-king verb, it is cold de "pre-di-keit no-mi-na-tiv."`

**Example: "God is love."**
`Eg-zam-pol: "God is lov."`

**In this sentence, the noun "love" is the predicate nominative.**
`In dis sen-tens, de naun "lov" is de pre-di-keit no-mi-na-tiv.`

**b. The objective case is using a noun (or pronoun) as an object in a sentence.**
`bi. De ob-yek-tiv keis is iu-sing a naun (or pro-naun) as an ob-yekt in a sen-tens.`

**That is, if a noun (or pronoun) receives the action of a transitive verb, that noun is in the objective case.** **(verbo transitivo)**
`Dat is, if a naun (or pro-naun) ri-sivs de ak-shon ov a tran-si-tiv verb, dat naun is in de ob-yek-tiv keis.`

**A transitive verb is a verb whose action carries over to another person, place, or thing.** **(se transfiere)**
`A tran-si-tiv verb is a verb jus ak-shon ca-ris o-ver tu a-no-der per-son, pleis, or ting.`

**The person, place, or thing upon which the action has an effect is "the object" of the verb.**
`De per-son, pleis, or ting a-pon wich de ak-shon has an e-fekt is "di ob-yekt" ov de verb.`

**Example: "John hit Mary."**
`Eg-zam-pol: "Yon hit Me-ri."`

**The noun "Mary" is the object of the verb "hit" and is, therefore, in the objective case.**
`De naun "Me-ri" is di ob-yekt ov de verb "hit" and is, der-for, in de ob-yek-tiv keis.`

**Or, if a noun (or pronoun) follows a preposition, it is in the objective case.**
`Or, if a naun (or pro-naun) fo-lous a pre-po-si-shon, it is in de ob-yek-tiv keis.`

**Example: "Mary went to town."**
`Eg-zam-pol: "Me-ri went tu taun."`

**The noun "town" is the object of the preposition "to" and is in the objective case.**
`De naun "taun" is di ob-yekt ov de pre-po-si-shon "tu" and is in de ob-yek-tiv keis.`

**c. The possessive case uses a noun (or pronoun) to show ownership or relationship.** **(posesión)** **(relación)**
`si. De po-se-siv keis iu-ses a naun (or pro-naun) tu shou ou-ner-ship or ri-lei-shon-ship.`

**Examples: Amy's toy, Carla's doll, Debbie's hair, Dan's saw, Matthew's shoes, Tina's book.** **(sierra)**
`Eg-zam-pols: Ei-mis toi, Car-las dol, De-bis jer, Dans so, Ma-tius shus, Ti-nas buk.`

**As we noticed in Lesson Two, a singular possessive noun is formed by adding an apostrophe (') and an "s" to the noun.**
`As wi no-tist in Le-son Tu, a sin-giu-lar po-se-siv naun is formd bai a-ding an a-pos-tro-fi (') and an "s" tu de naun.`

**Examples: boy's hat, girl's bicycle, teacher's book, citizen's rights.** **(derechos del ciudadano)**
`Eg-zam-pols: bois hat, gerls bai-si-kol, ti-chers buk, ci-ti-sens raits.`

**A plural possessive noun ending in "s" is formed by adding the apostrophe only.**
`A plu-ral po-se-siv naun en-ding in "s" is formd bai a-ding di a-pos-tro-fi on-li.`

**Examples: boys' hats, girls' bicycles, teachers' books, citizens' rights.**
`Eg-zam-pols: bois hats, gerls bai-si-kols, ti-chers buks, ci-ti-sens raits.`
